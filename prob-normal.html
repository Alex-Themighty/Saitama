1. Theory & Mathematical Foundation

The Normal Distribution $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ is the most important distribution in statistics. Because every distribution has a different mean and spread, we use Standardization to calculate probabilities consistently.

Standard Normal ($Z$)

Fixed: $\mu=0, \sigma=1$

General Normal ($X$)

Variable data shift/stretch

  1. The Standard Distribution ($Z$): A special case where $Z \sim N(0, 1^2)$. It serves as the universal benchmark for all normal data.
  2. The Z-Score (The Transformation): $Z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$

    This formula translates any raw value ($x$) into "number of standard deviations from the mean." If $Z=2$, your value is exactly 2 standard deviations above the average.

  3. Obtaining Probabilities: Probability corresponds to the Area Under the Curve. The total area is always $1.0$. We use the Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) to find the area to the left of a Z-score.
  4. Inverse Probability (Finding $x$): If given a probability (area), we find the corresponding $Z$ value from a table (or calculator) and solve for $x$: $x = \mu + Z\sigma$.
